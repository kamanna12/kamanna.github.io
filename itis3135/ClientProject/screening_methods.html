Screening Methods

Tests are used to screen for different types of cancer when a person does not have symptoms. Three screening tests have been studied to see if they decrease the risk of dying from lung cancer.

  • Low-dose Computed Tomography (LDCT)

    A procedure that uses low-dose radiation to make a series of very detailed pictures of areas inside the body using an x-ray machine that scans the body in a spiral path. This procedure is also called spiral scan or helical scan.

    Pros: Effective at finding early-stage lung cancer.

    Cons: False-positive results may lead to unnecessary invasive procedures, overdiagnosis of some lung cancers.

  • Chest X-ray

    An x-ray of the organs and bones inside the chest.

    Pros: Simple and non-invasive.

    Cons: Less effective at detecting early-stage lung cancer compared to LDCT, may result in false-negative and false-positive results.

  • Sputum Cytology

    A procedure in which a sample of sputum (mucus that is coughed up from the lungs) is viewed under a microscope to check for cancer cells.

    Pros: Non-invasive.

    Cons: Less effective than LDCT at detecting early-stage lung cancer, may result in false-negative and false-positive results.

How Screening is Done

Screening for lung cancer is done with a yearly low-dose CT scan (LDCT). For this test, you lie on a thin, flat table that slides back and forth inside the hole in the middle of the CT scanner, which is a large, doughnut-shaped machine.

As the table moves into the opening, an x-ray tube rotates within the scanner, sending out many tiny x-ray beams at precise angles. These beams quickly pass through your body and are detected on the other side of the scanner. A computer then converts these results into detailed images of the lungs.

An LDCT scan is painless and only takes a few minutes, although the entire visit (including getting you ready and into place on the table) can take up to half an hour. If something abnormal is found during screening, more CT scans or other tests will be needed to be sure. Some of these tests are described in Tests for Lung Cancer.

CT scans of the lungs can also sometimes show problems in other organs near the lungs. Your doctor will discuss any such findings with you if they are found.
